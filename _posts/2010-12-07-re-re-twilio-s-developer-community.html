I wanted to drop a note to see how your experience with Twilio has been going. I see you've had a play around, and I hope you were impressed with how easy building voice and SMS applications can be using Twilio.

I also wanted to offer some tips and tricks that other Twilio customers have found useful, and answer some frequent questions new developers have:

Develop in Your Browser

The usual first step to testing a web app is to load it in your browser, and see if it looks right, make sure there are no errors, etc. Right? That's also the easiest way to start developing a Twilio app... build and test in your browser! Load up your application's URL in your browser, and check to see that it's returning valid, error-free XML. Using an XML validating browser, such as Firefox, is really helpful. You can also simulate input to your web app that are normally generated by Twilio, and ensure your application responds correctly. For example, if you use to receive keypad digits from the caller, Twilio will send a "Digits" parameter to that handler URL indicating what keys were pressed. You could simulate a caller pressing "1" by entering this URL in your browser: http://my-application.com/my-handler?Digits=1

Scaling Is Easy and Transparent

Twilio doesn't limit how many calls you can make or receive at the same time. Many traditional telecom companies charge you for by the "channel" or "trunk", essentially limiting how many people can interact with your application. We thought that was stupid, so Twilio just charges you per minute for calls, or per-message for SMS... so grow at your own pace and leave the scaling up to us.

We Host the Audio

When you generate call recordings using the <Record> verb, the resulting recordings are reliably hosted on Twilio's servers... you never have to worry about storing binary audio on your end, just reference the RecordingUrl in your application. Store the RecordingUrl in your database... Drop it into a <Play> tag in another call... Embed the recording in a webpage as an MP3!
